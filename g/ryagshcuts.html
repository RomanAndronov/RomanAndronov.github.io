g Shortcuts

While any Euclidean straight edge and compass construction can be documented with \(g\), it may produce a large number of intermediate objects which, however useful, are overall irrelevant. In order to not pollute such a construction with these types of objects it is possible to omit them altogether by using \(g\) shortcuts which are compound statements that, while lacking all the required details, still capture the essence of the operation at hand.

For example, in Ruler and Compass 02 section we document the steps for constructing perpendiculars and parallels. Here is a full 3-step construction of a perpendicular to a given straight line \(l\) through a given point \(A\) on \(l\):

$$Cir(O \notin l, OA) \cap l = A, B$$ $$Ln(B, O) \cap Cir(O, OA) = B, C$$ $$Ln(C, A) \bot l$$

We see that in addition to two given objects we generate five more: three points, a circle and a straight line. Since the sole objective of this construction is to demonstrate exactly how to solve a given problem, we have no choice but enumerate all the steps - without them there is no solution.

However, if constructing a perpendicular through a point on a straight line is just an intermediate step in a larger solution of a bigger problem and the above mentioned five intermediate objects are not used during the remaining steps it is permissible to compress the above steps into a shortcut.

We can record this shortcut as:

$$C \notin l \colon Ln(A \in l, C) \bot l$$

which formally reads as:

construct a point \(C\) not on \(l\) such that a straight line passing through \(A\) on \(l\) and through \(C\) is perpendicular to \(l\)

Such shortcuts, however, will never be used in a vacuum - there will always be enough context in the course of a given construction to unambiguously place or locate the shortcut's objects. As such, it is permissible to compress most of the shortcuts even further. For example:

$$C \colon Ln(A, C) = p \bot l$$

means:

construct a perpendicular \(p\) to \(l\) through \(A\)

To construct an arbitrary circle through \(A\) perpendicular to \(l\), we may use the following shortcut:

$$O \colon Cir(O, OA) = p \bot l$$

meaning:

construct a circle \(p\) perpendicular to \(l\) through \(A\)

and so on.

We see that a shortcut captures exactly what is being constructed in the smallest and most economical way possible without sacrificing the geometrical correctness of the process. However, at any time it is possible to inflate any shortcut and expose all of its underlying details.

Here are several more shortcuts for popular primitive constructions.

A perpendicular to a straight line through a point not on it:

$$D \colon Ln(A \notin l, D) = p \bot l$$

or simply:

$$D \colon Ln(A, D) = p \bot l$$

A parallel to a straight line through a point:

$$E \colon Ln(A, E) \| l$$

A perpendicular bisector to a line segment:

$$C, D, E \colon Ln(C, D) \bot Ln(A, B), AE = EB$$

An angle bisector:

$$D \subset \angle ABC \colon \angle ABD = \angle CBD$$

A tangent to a circle through a point \(A\) not on the circumference of that circle:

$$T \in Cir(O, r) \colon Ln(A \not\subset Cir(O, r), T) \bot Cir(O, r)$$

or:

$$T \colon Ln(A, T) = p \bot Cir(O, r)$$

and so on.

\(\blacksquare\)
