Power Sums

In this chapter we will present a uniform approach to computing the magnitudes of the following power sums:

$$\sum_{i = 1}^n i^p, \quad p \in [1, 2, 3, \dots]$$

in closed form.

The main idea of this approach is the following. Assume that \(p\) is the power of the sequence in question. Instead of staying and working with that power alone we shall consider the expansion of a specific sum of power that is one greater than \(p\) which gives the name to the approach that solves the problem:

$$\begin{equation} (n + 1)^{p + 1} \end{equation}$$

We then will construct \(n - 1\) more equations by reducing \(n\) in (1) by one every time. Thus we will obtain \(n\) equations which we will add together and observe that the majority of the intermediate terms will cancel out leaving us with a linear equation in \(S_p\) expressed in terms of \(S_{p-1}, S_{p-2}, S_{p-3}\) and so on.

One obvious consequence of this approach is that the sums of lower powers must be computed first. Let us see how exactly does it work.


First Powers

Find the sum of first powers of first \(n\) natural numbers in closed form:

$$\begin{equation} S_1 = \sum_{i = 1}^n i \end{equation}$$


Solution

Starting with (3):

$$\begin{equation} (n + 1)^2 = n^2 + 2n + 1 \end{equation}$$

construct \(n - 1\) more equations by replacing \(n\) in (3) with, in turn, \(n - 1\), \(n - 2\), \(n - 3\) and so on until \(1\) is reached:

$$\begin{equation} +\,\left\{\begin{array}{rcllll} (n + 1)^2 & = & n^2 & + & 2n & + & 1 \\ n^2 & = & (n - 1)^2 & + & 2(n - 1) & + & 1 \\ (n - 1)^2 & = & (n - 2)^2 & + & 2(n - 2) & + & 1 \\ (n - 2)^2 & = & (n - 3)^2 & + & 2(n - 3) & + & 1 \\ \dots & = & \dots & + & \dots & + & 1 \\ 2^2 & = & 1^2 & + & 2 \times 1 & + & 1 \end{array}\right. \end{equation}$$

We observe that diagonally, North-East to South-West, the first term on the right hand side of the equal sign in all the equations will cancel out with the only term on the left hand side of the equation below.

For example, the term \(n^2\) on the right hand side of the equal sign of the first equation will cancel out with the only term \(n^2\) on the left hand side of the second equation.

After all such cancellations (4) will collapse into a single first order equation in \(S_1\):

$$(n + 1)^2 = 1^2 + 2 \underbrace{(n + (n - 1) + (n - 2) + \ldots + 1)}_{S_1} + \underbrace{1 + 1 + \dots + 1}_{n} =$$ $$1 + 2S_1 + n$$

solving which for \(S_1\) we obtain:

$$2S_1 = (n + 1)^2 - (n + 1) =$$ $$(n + 1)(n + 1 - 1) =$$ $$(n + 1)n$$

and:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{S_1 = \sum_{i = 1}^n i = \frac {n(n + 1)}{2}} \end{equation}$$


Second Powers

Find the sum of second powers of first \(n\) natural numbers:

$$\begin{equation} S_2 = \sum_{i = i}^n i^2 \end{equation}$$


Solution

Starting with (7):

$$\begin{equation} (n + 1)^3 = n^3 + 3n^2 + 3n + 1 \end{equation}$$

construct \(n - 1\) more equations by replacing \(n\) in (7) with, in turn, \(n - 1\), \(n - 2\), \(n - 3\) and so on until \(1\) is reached:

$$\begin{equation} +\,\left\{\begin{array}{rcllllll} (n + 1)^3 & = & n^3 & + & 3n^2 & + & 3n & + & 1 \\ n^3 & = & (n - 1)^3 & + & 3(n - 1)^2 & + & 3(n - 1) & + & 1 \\ (n - 1)^3 & = & (n - 2)^3 & + & 3(n - 2)^2 & + & 3(n - 2) & + & 1 \\ (n - 2)^3 & = & (n - 3)^3 & + & 3(n - 3)^2 & + & 3(n - 3) & + & 1 \\ \dots & = & \dots & + & \dots & + & \dots & + & 1 \\ 2^3 & = & 1^3 & + & 3 \times 1^2 & + & 3 \times 1 & + & 1 \end{array}\right. \end{equation}$$

After the diagonal cancellation that we have described in the previous section, (8) will collapse into a linear equation in \(S_2\) expressed in terms of \(S_1\) the magnitude of which in closed form we have computed already:

$$\begin{equation} (n + 1)^3 = 1 + 3S_2 + 3S_1 + n \end{equation}$$

Solving (9) for \(S_2\) using (5) for \(S_1\) we obtain:

$$3S_2 = (n + 1)^3 - (n + 1) - \frac {3n(n + 1)}{2} =$$ $$(n + 1)\Big((n + 1)^2 - 1 - \frac {3n}{2}\Big) =$$ $$(n + 1)\Big(n^2 + 2n + 1 - 1 - \frac {3n}{2}\Big) =$$ $$(n + 1)\Big(n^2 + \frac {n}{2}\Big) =$$ $$n(n + 1)\Big(n + \frac {1}{2}\Big)$$

and:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{S_2 = \sum_{i = i}^n i^2 = \frac {n(n + 1)(2n + 1)}{6}} \end{equation}$$


Third Powers

Find the sum of third powers of first \(n\) natural numbers:

$$S_3 = \sum_{i = 1}^n i^3$$


Solution

After computing the first and the second powers using the method described, we instantly recognize the collapsing pattern by looking at (3) and (7) and the corresponding equations into which (4) and (8) are transformed.

Consequently, to compute the sums of the remaining powers we do not even have to construct the system of equations any more. All we have to do is use the binomial expansion of \((n + 1)^{p + 1}\):

$$\begin{equation} (n + 1)^4 = n^4 + 4n^3 + 6n^2 + 4n + 1 \end{equation}$$

In (11) replace the first term, \(n^{p + 1}\), with \(1\), the last term, \(1\), with \(n\) and the remaining powers of \(n\) with the corresponding sum \(S_p, S_{p - 1}, S_{p - 2}\), etc:

$$(n + 1)^4 = 1 + 4S_3 + 6S_2 + 4S_1 + n$$

and solve the resulting equation substituting the known closed-form expressions for each intermediate sum:

$$4S_3 = (n + 1)^4 - 1 - 6S_2 - 4S_1 - n =$$ $$(n + 1)^4 - 1 - n(n + 1)(2n + 1) - 2n(n + 1) - n =$$ $$(n + 1)((n + 1)^3 - n(2n + 1) - 2n - 1) =$$ $$(n + 1)((n + 1)^3 - (2n + 1)(n + 1)) =$$ $$(n + 1)^2((n + 1)^2 - 2n - 1) =$$ $$(n + 1)^2(n^2 + 2n + 1 - 2n - 1) =$$ $$n^2(n + 1)^2$$

to obtain the answer:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{S_3 = \sum_{i = 1}^n i^3 = \frac {n^2(n + 1)^2}{4} = S_1^2} \end{equation}$$


Higher Powers

As an exercise, compute the sums of fourth, fifths and sixth powers:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{S_4 = \sum_{i = 1}^n i^4 = \frac {n(n + 1)(2n + 1)(3n^2 + 3n - 1)}{30}} \end{equation}$$ $$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{S_5 = \sum_{i = 1}^n i^5 = \frac {n^2(n + 1)^2(2n^2 + 2n - 1)}{12}} \end{equation}$$ $$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{S_6 = \sum_{i = 1}^n i^6 = \frac {n(n + 1)(2n + 1)(3n^4 + 6n^3 - 3n + 1)}{42}} \end{equation}$$

\(\blacksquare\)
