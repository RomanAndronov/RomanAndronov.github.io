Armies

Problem

Two armies, \(A\) and \(B\), are engaged in a direct combat in the open within direct view of each other. Initially the army \(A\) has \(a_0\) soldiers while the army \(B\) has \(b_0\) soldiers and:

$$a_0 > b_0$$

1) How many soldiers will the army \(A\) be left with by the time the army \(B\) is totally annihilated assuming a continuous attrition with constant rates for both armies?

2) How long will it take the army \(A\) to totally annihilate the army \(B\)?


Solution

Assuming a discrete time combat model for a moment, let \(a(n)\) and \(b(n)\) be the number of soldiers the armies \(A\) and \(B\) have correspondingly at a moment of time \(n\). We then model the number of soldiers each army has standing one unit of time later as a difference between the current number of soldiers and the losses each army suffered after the current fire exchange:

$$a(n + 1) = a(n) - l_a(n)$$ $$b(n + 1) = b(n) - l_b(n)$$

where \(l_a(n)\) stands for the losses of the army \(A\) inflicted by the army \(B\) - the number of soldiers of the army \(A\) annihilated by the soldiers of the army \(B\) after one unit of time has passed.

Correspondingly, \(l_b(n)\) is the number of soldiers of the army \(B\) annihilated by the soldiers of the army \(A\) during the same unit of time.

Next, we assume that:

$$l_a(n) = \beta \times 1 \times b(n)$$ $$l_b(n) = \alpha \times 1 \times a(n)$$

where \(\beta\) is the number of soldiers (of the army \(A\)) one soldier of the army \(B\) can annihilate per unit of time, \(1\), and \(\alpha\) is the number of soldiers (of the army \(B\)) one soldier of the army \(A\) can annihilate per unit of time, also \(1\):

$$a(n + 1) = a(n) - \beta \times 1 \times b(n)$$ $$b(n + 1) = b(n) - \alpha \times 1 \times a(n)$$

In other words, we assume that:

- all the soldiers of a given army have the same lethal skill captured in \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\)

- \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are both constant over time, to be sure the units of measurement of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are "soldiers/time"

- the losses inflicted by each army on its opponent depend only on the number of standing soldiers and their lethal skill or offensive fire effectiveness - we explicitly do not account for the (natural) defensive mechanisms

- the losing army is willing to fight till the bitter end despite facing certain and inevitable annihilation

- the winning army is willing to fight till the not bitter end despite facing certain and inevitable victory

Moving from a discrete to a continuous flow of time we take it that after \(\Delta t\) amount of time of the above combat elapses, the losses suffered by each army become:

$$a(t + \Delta t) - a(t) = - \beta \Delta t b(t)$$ $$b(t + \Delta t) - b(t) = - \alpha \Delta t a(t)$$

Divide both of the above equations by \(\Delta t\):

$$\frac {a(t + \Delta t) - a(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac {- \beta \Delta t b(t)}{\Delta t} =- \beta b(t)$$ $$\frac {b(t + \Delta t) - b(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac {- \alpha \Delta a(t)}{\Delta t} = - \alpha a(t)$$

and apply a limit to them when \(\Delta t \to 0\):

$$\lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac {a(t + \Delta t) - a(t)}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} - \beta b(t)$$ $$\lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac {b(t + \Delta t) - b(t)}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} - \alpha a(t)$$

On the left of the equal sign in each equation we have a derivative - a speed of man power loss. On the right hand of the equal sign in each equation we have a function that does not depend on \(\Delta t\):

$$\begin{equation} \frac {da(t)}{dt} = -\beta b(t) \end{equation}$$ $$\begin{equation} \frac {db(t)}{dt} = -\alpha b(t) \end{equation}$$


1)

Divide (1) by (2):

$$\frac {da(t)}{db(t)} = \frac {\beta b(t)}{\alpha a(t)}$$

and rearrange the result:

$$\begin{equation} a(t)da = \frac {\beta}{\alpha} b(t)db \end{equation}$$

Since the varialbes in (3) are separated, integrate both sides of it:

$$\int_{a_0}^{a(t)}a(t)da = \int_{b_0}^{b(t)} \frac {\beta}{\alpha} b(t)db$$ $$\begin{equation} a^2(t) - a_0^2 = \frac {\beta}{\alpha}\Big(b^2(t) - b_0^2\Big) \end{equation}$$

We are told that at a certain point of time \(t = t_a\) the army \(B\) has no more soldiers left:

$$b(t_a) = 0$$

Substituting the above value for \(b(t)\) in (4), we obtain:

$$a^2(t_a) - a_0^2 = \frac {\beta}{\alpha}\Big(0 - b_0^2\Big)$$

from where it follows that the number of soldiers left in the army \(A\) by the time the army \(B\) is totally annihilated is:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{a(b = 0) = \sqrt{a_0^2 - \frac {\beta}{\alpha}b_0^2}} \end{equation}$$


2)

Here we need to find \(a\) and \(b\) as the acutal functions of time, \(a(t)\) and \(b(t)\).

Take the first derivative of (1) over time:

$$\begin{equation} \frac {d^2a}{dt^2} = -\beta \frac {db}{dt} \end{equation}$$

Take the first derivative of \(b(t)\) from (2) and put it into (6):

$$\begin{equation} \frac {d^2a}{dt^2} = -\beta \times (- \alpha a) = \alpha \beta a \end{equation}$$ $$\begin{equation} \frac {d^2a}{dt^2} = \alpha \beta a \end{equation}$$

Temporarily, to save us some typing, substitue:

$$\begin{equation} \alpha \beta = \omega^2 \end{equation}$$ $$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{\frac {d^2a}{dt^2} = \omega^2 a} \end{equation}$$

Purely mathematically this equation is the same as the mooftochka's equation:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{\frac {d^2x}{dt^2} = \omega^2x}$$

Over here \(a(t)\) is the number of surviving troops, over there it is a linear coordinate of a material point. The equations are fundamentally the same but the solutions come out a bit different becaue of different initial conditions in the direct flow of time. We divide (1) by (2):

$$\begin{equation} \frac {da}{db} = \frac {\beta b}{\alpha a} \end{equation}$$

Rearrange (8) to put the like variables on the same side:

$$\begin{equation} ada = \frac {\beta}{\alpha} bd \end{equation}$$

Inetgrate both sides of (9):

$$\begin{equation} a^2 + C = \frac {\beta}{\alpha} b^2 \end{equation}$$

To find \(C\) we use the initial conditions:

$$a(0) = A_0$$ $$b(0) = B_0$$ $$C = \frac {\beta}{\alpha} B_0^2 - A_0^2$$

Express \(a\) in terms of \(b\) in (10):

$$\begin{equation} a = \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha} b^2 - C} \end{equation}$$

Put (11) back into (2):

$$\begin{equation} \frac {db}{dt} = -\alpha \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha} b^2 - C} = -\sqrt{\alpha \beta}\sqrt{b^2 - \frac{\alpha}{\beta}C} \end{equation}$$

Rearrange (12) to put the like variables on the same side:

$$\frac {db}{\sqrt{b^2 - \frac{\alpha}{\beta}C}} = -\sqrt{\alpha \beta} dt$$ $$K^2 = \frac{\alpha}{\beta}C$$ $$\frac {db}{\sqrt{b^2 - K^2}} = -\sqrt{\alpha \beta} dt$$ $$\int_{B_0}^b \frac {db}{\sqrt{b^2 - K^2}} = \int_0^t -\sqrt{\alpha \beta} dt = -\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t$$

To calculate the integral on the left we use the \(b = K \cos ^{-1}(z)\) substitution:

$$db = \frac {K \sin(z)}{\cos^2(z)}dz$$ $$\sqrt{b^2 - K^2} = K \frac {\sqrt{1 - \cos^2(z)}}{\cos(z)} = \frac {K \sin(z)}{\cos(z)}$$ $$\int \frac {db}{\sqrt{b^2 - K^2}} = \int \frac {K \sin(z)}{\cos^2(z)} \frac {\cos(z)}{K \sin(z)} dz = \int \frac {dz}{\cos(z)}$$ $$\int \frac {dz}{\cos(z)} = \int \frac {1}{\cos(z)} \frac {\frac {1}{\cos(z)} + \tan(z)}{\frac {1}{\cos(z)} + \tan(z)} dz =$$ $$= \int \frac {\frac {1}{\cos^2(z)} + \frac {1}{\cos(z)}\tan(z)}{\frac {1}{\cos(z)} + \tan(z)}dz$$ $$u = \frac {1}{\cos(z)} + \tan(z)$$ $$du = \Big(\frac {1}{\cos^2(z)} + \frac {1}{\cos(z)}\tan(z)\Big) dz$$ $$\int \frac {dz}{\cos(z)} = \int \frac {du}{u} = \ln(u) + C =$$ $$= \ln\Big(\frac {1}{\cos(z)} + \tan(z)\Big) + C$$ $$\frac {1}{\cos(z)} = \frac {b}{K}$$ $$\tan(z) = \frac {\sqrt{1 - \cos^2(z)}}{\cos(z)} =$$ $$= \frac {\cos(z)\sqrt{\frac{1}{\cos^2(z)} - 1}}{\cos(z)} =$$ $$= \sqrt{\frac{b^2}{K^2} - 1} = \frac {1}{K} \sqrt{b^2 - K^2}$$ $$\int \frac {dz}{\cos(z)} = \ln\Big(\frac {b}{K} + \frac {1}{K} \sqrt{b^2 - K^2}\Big) =$$ $$= \ln(b + \sqrt{b^2 - K^2}) - \ln(K) + const$$ $$\int_{B_0}^b \frac {db}{\sqrt{b^2 - K^2}} = \ln(b + \sqrt{b^2 - K^2}) - \ln(B_0 + \sqrt{B_0^2 - K^2})$$ $$\sqrt{B_0^2 - K^2} = \sqrt{B_0^2 - B_0^2 + \frac {\alpha}{\beta}A_0^2} = \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0$$ $$\frac {b + \sqrt{b^2 - K^2}}{B_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0} = e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t}$$ $$b + \sqrt{b^2 - K^2} = (B_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0)e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t}$$ $$b - (B_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0)e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} = -\sqrt{b^2 - K^2}$$

Square both sides:

$$b^2 - 2b(B_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0)e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} + (B_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0)^2e^{-2\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} = b^2 - K^2$$ $$(B_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0)^2e^{-2\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} + K^2 = 2b(B_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0)e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t}$$ $$K^2 = B_0^2 - \frac {\alpha}{\beta}A_0^2 = (B_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0) \times (B_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0)$$ $$b(t) = \frac {1}{2}((\sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0 + B_0)e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} + (B_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0)e^{\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t})$$ $$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{b(t) = \frac {1}{2}\sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}((A_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0)e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} - (A_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0)e^{\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t})} \end{equation}$$

From (2) it follows that for \(a(t)\) we have:

$$a(t) = - \frac {1}{\alpha} \frac {db(t)}{dt}$$

Or from (1) it follows that for \(a(t)\) we have:

$$a(t) = - \beta \int b(t)dt$$

Either way we get:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{a(t) = \frac {1}{2}((A_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0)e^{- \sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} + (A_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0)e^{\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t})} \end{equation}$$

It follows then that we can have either \(a(t) = 0\) or \(b(t) = 0\). If \(a(T) = 0\) then from (14) it follows that:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{T(a = 0) = \frac {1}{2\sqrt{\alpha \beta}} \ln\Big(- \frac {A_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0}{A_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0}\Big)} \end{equation}$$

If \(b(T) = 0\) from (13) it follows that:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{T(b = 0) = \frac {1}{2\sqrt{\alpha \beta}} \ln\Big(\frac {A_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0}{A_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0}\Big)} \end{equation}$$

So which one works? From (11) we have:

$$a = \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha} b^2 - C} = \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha} b^2 - \frac {\beta}{\alpha} B_0^2 + A_0^2}$$ $$\begin{equation} a = \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}b^2 + A_0^2 - \frac {\beta}{\alpha}B_0^2} \end{equation}$$

From this it follows that if the army \(B\) gets annihilated then \(b = 0\) and from (17) we have the number of troops of the army \(A\) still standing:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{a(b = 0) = \sqrt{A_0^2 - \frac {\beta}{\alpha}B_0^2}} \end{equation}$$

From which it follows that if the number of surviving troops to be Real and not Complex then the expression under the square root must be greater than zero:

$$A_0 > \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}B_0$$

Then (15) is not defined and (16) is the answer for the time question - how long does it take for the army \(A\) to totally annihilate the army \(B\). If, however, we say that the army \(B\) is more numerous then we put \(a == 0\) in (17) and:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{b(a = 0) = \sqrt{B_0^2 - \frac {\alpha}{\beta}A_0^2}} \end{equation}$$

Then the \(\ln()\) in (16) is not defined because for number of troops to remain Real:

$$B_0 > \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}A_0$$

and the answer for \(T\) is (15).

Putting the equations and their solutions side by side we can correlate the number of the surviving troops of the winning army (say \(A\)) to the radius \(x\) of the mooftochka problem and we can correlate the troops of the losing army (say \(B\)) to mooftochka's radial velocity:

$$\frac {d^2a}{dt^2} = \alpha \beta a \qquad a(b = 0) = \sqrt{A_0^2 - \frac {\beta}{\alpha}B_0^2}$$ $$\frac {d^2x}{dt^2} = \omega^2x \qquad v_x(x = L) = \omega\sqrt{L^2 - L_0^2}$$

In the armies problem \(a(t)\) and \(b(t)\) diminish with time while in the mooftochka problem \(x(t)\) and \(v_x(t)\) grow with time. To make the two problems mathematically equiavalent we reverse the flow of time for either problem and claim that \(A_0\) corresponds to \(x = L\), \(a(b = 0)\) corresponds to \(x = L_0\) and \(B_0\) corresponds to the radial velocity \(v_x(x = L)\). We now have to make numerically:

$$\alpha = \beta = 1 = \omega$$

and then for the mooftochka problem we translate the equation from the armies problem:

$$(a^2(b = 0) \equiv L_0^2) = (A_0^2 \equiv L^2) - (B_0^2 \equiv v_x^2)$$ $$L_0^2 = L^2 - v_x^2$$ $$v_x = \sqrt{L^2 - L_0^2}$$

\(\blacksquare\)
