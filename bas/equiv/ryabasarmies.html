Armies

(I was alerted to this and the next, Roliding Bead, problem by my grade school, college and life-time friend, Michael Batygov)

Problem

Two armies, \(A\) and \(B\), are engaged in a direct combat in the open within direct view of each other. Initially the army \(A\) has \(a_0\) soldiers while the army \(B\) has \(b_0\) soldiers and:

$$a_0 > b_0$$

1) How many soldiers will the army \(A\) be left with by the time the army \(B\) is totally annihilated assuming a continuous attrition with constant rates for both armies?

2) How long will it take the army \(A\) to totally annihilate the army \(B\)?


Solution

Assuming a discrete time combat model for a moment, let \(a(n)\) and \(b(n)\) be the number of soldiers the armies \(A\) and \(B\) have correspondingly at a moment of time \(n\). We then model the number of soldiers each army has standing one unit of time later as a difference between the current number of soldiers and the losses each army suffered after the current fire exchange:

$$a(n + 1) = a(n) - l_a(n)$$ $$b(n + 1) = b(n) - l_b(n)$$

where \(l_a(n)\) stands for the losses of the army \(A\) inflicted by the army \(B\) - the number of soldiers of the army \(A\) annihilated by the soldiers of the army \(B\) after one unit of time has passed.

Correspondingly, \(l_b(n)\) is the number of soldiers of the army \(B\) annihilated by the soldiers of the army \(A\) during the same unit of time.

Next, we assume that:

$$l_a(n) = \beta \times 1 \times b(n)$$ $$l_b(n) = \alpha \times 1 \times a(n)$$

where \(\beta\) is the number of soldiers (of the army \(A\)) one soldier of the army \(B\) can annihilate per unit of time, \(1\), and \(\alpha\) is the number of soldiers (of the army \(B\)) one soldier of the army \(A\) can annihilate per unit of time, also \(1\):

$$a(n + 1) = a(n) - \beta \times 1 \times b(n)$$ $$b(n + 1) = b(n) - \alpha \times 1 \times a(n)$$

In other words, we assume that:

- all the soldiers of a given army have the same lethal skill captured in \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\)

- \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are both constant over time, to be sure the units of measurement of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are "soldiers/time"

- the losses inflicted by each army on its opponent depend only on the number of standing soldiers and their lethal skill or offensive fire effectiveness - we explicitly do not account for the (natural) defensive mechanisms

- the losing army is willing to fight till the bitter end despite facing certain and inevitable annihilation

- the winning army is willing to fight till the not bitter end despite facing certain and inevitable victory

Moving from a discrete to a continuous flow of time we take it that after \(\Delta t\) amount of time of the above combat elapses, the losses suffered by each army become:

$$a(t + \Delta t) - a(t) = - \beta \Delta t b(t)$$ $$b(t + \Delta t) - b(t) = - \alpha \Delta t a(t)$$

Divide both of the above equations by \(\Delta t\):

$$\frac {a(t + \Delta t) - a(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac {- \beta \Delta t b(t)}{\Delta t} =- \beta b(t)$$ $$\frac {b(t + \Delta t) - b(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac {- \alpha \Delta a(t)}{\Delta t} = - \alpha a(t)$$

and apply a limit to them when \(\Delta t \to 0\):

$$\lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac {a(t + \Delta t) - a(t)}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} - \beta b(t)$$ $$\lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac {b(t + \Delta t) - b(t)}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} - \alpha a(t)$$

On the left of the equal sign in each equation we have a derivative - a speed of man power loss. On the right hand of the equal sign in each equation we have a function that does not depend on \(\Delta t\):

$$\begin{equation} \frac {da}{dt} = -\beta b \end{equation}$$ $$\begin{equation} \frac {db}{dt} = -\alpha a \end{equation}$$


1) A-army survivors

Divide (1) by (2):

$$\frac {da}{db} = \frac {\beta b}{\alpha a}$$

and rearrange the result:

$$\begin{equation} a \; da = \frac {\beta}{\alpha} b \; db \end{equation}$$

Since the variables in (3) are separated, integrate both sides of it:

$$\int_{a_0}^a a \; da = \int_{b_0}^b \frac {\beta}{\alpha} b \; db$$ $$a^2 - a_0^2 = \frac {\beta}{\alpha}\Big(b^2 - b_0^2\Big)$$ $$\begin{equation} a(b) = \sqrt{a_0^2 + \frac {\beta}{\alpha}\Big(b^2 - b_0^2\Big)} \end{equation}$$

where we have kept only the positive value of the square root as the number of troops can not be negative.

We are told that at a certain point of time \(t = t_a\) the army \(B\) has no more soldiers left:

$$b(t_a) = 0$$

Substituting the above value for \(b\) into (4), we obtain the number of troops left in the army \(A\) by the time the army \(B\) is totally annihilated:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{a(b = 0) = \sqrt{a_0^2 - \frac {\beta}{\alpha}b_0^2}} \end{equation}$$

The above answer in particular and the pair of differential equations in general are known as Lanchester's Square Law.


2) Annihilation Time

Here we need to find \(a\) and \(b\) as the actual functions of time, \(a(t)\) and \(b(t)\).

Substitute the value of \(a(b)\) from (4) into (2):

$$\begin{equation} \frac {db}{dt} = -\alpha \sqrt{a_0^2 + \frac {\beta}{\alpha}\Big(b^2 - b_0^2\Big)} \end{equation}$$

Rearrange the expression under the square root:

$$\begin{equation} \frac {db}{dt} = - \sqrt{\alpha \beta} \sqrt{b^2 + \frac {\alpha}{\beta}a_0^2 - b_0^2} \end{equation}$$

and, since the following is a constant, temporarily put:

$$\begin{equation} k^2 = \frac {\alpha}{\beta}a_0^2 - b_0^2 \end{equation}$$

Then (7) becomes:

$$\begin{equation} \frac {db}{dt} = - \sqrt{\alpha \beta} \sqrt{b^2 + k^2} \end{equation}$$

Preparing for integration, separate the variables \(b\) and \(t\) in (9):

$$\begin{equation} \frac {db}{\sqrt{b^2 + k^2}} = - \sqrt{\alpha \beta} \; dt \end{equation}$$

Integrate both sides of (10): the left side from \(b_0\) to \(b\) and the right side from \(0\) to \(t\):

$$\begin{equation} \int_{b_0}^b \frac {db}{\sqrt{b^2 + k^2}} = - \sqrt{\alpha \beta} \; t \end{equation}$$

Compute the integral in (11) using the method of substitution. In general, hyperbolic sine and cosine are profitable substitutions for these types of integrals and for this one in particular hyperbolic sine is the function of choice:

$$\begin{equation} b = k \frac {e^x - e^{-x}}{2} \end{equation}$$

Differentiating (12), we have:

$$\begin{equation} db = k \frac {e^x + e^{-x}}{2} \end{equation}$$

and using (12) to compute the square root in the denominator of the integral in (11) we obtain:

$$\sqrt{b^2 + k^2} = \sqrt{k^2 \frac {e^{2x} - 2 + e^{-2x}}{4} + k^2} =$$ $$\frac{k}{2}\sqrt{e^{2x} + 2 + e^{-2x}} =$$ $$\frac{k}{2}\sqrt{(e^x + e^{-x})^2} =$$ $$\begin{equation} k \frac {e^x + e^{-x}}{2} \end{equation}$$

Putting (14) and (13) into the integral in (11), it collapses into:

$$\begin{equation} \int \frac {db}{\sqrt{b^2 + k^2}} = \int \frac {k}{2} \frac {2}{k} \frac {e^x + e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}} \; dx = \int dx = x \end{equation}$$

where we have omitted the boundaries of integration to avoid the clutter.

To express \(x\) in terms of \(b\) multiply both sides of (12) by \(e^x\):

$$\frac {2b}{k} e^x = e^{2x} - 1$$ $$\begin{equation} e^{2x} - \frac {2b}{k}e^x - 1 = 0 \end{equation}$$

(16) is a quadratic equation with respect to \(e^x\). Solving this equation for \(e^x\), we obtain:

$$\begin{equation} e^x = \frac {b}{k} + \sqrt{\frac {b^2}{k^2} + 1} \end{equation}$$

where we have kept only one of two roots since the exponential function in question must remain not negative.

From (17) it follows that:

$$\begin{equation} x(b) = \ln \Bigg( \frac {b}{k} + \sqrt{\frac {b^2}{k^2} + 1}\Bigg) \end{equation}$$

Putting (18) into (15) and using the original integration boundaries, we obtain for \(b\):

$$\ln \Bigg( \frac {b}{k} + \sqrt{\frac {b^2}{k^2} + 1}\Bigg) - \ln \Bigg( \frac {b_0}{k} + \sqrt{\frac {b_0^2}{k^2} + 1}\Bigg) =$$ $$\ln \Bigg(\frac {\frac {b}{k} + \sqrt{\frac {b^2}{k^2} + 1}} {\frac {b_0}{k} + \sqrt{\frac {b_0^2}{k^2} + 1}} \Bigg) =$$ $$\begin{equation} \ln \Bigg(\frac {b + \sqrt{b^2 + k^2}}{b_0 + \sqrt{b_0^2 + k^2}}\Bigg) = -\sqrt{\alpha \beta} \; t \end{equation}$$

Putting the expression for \(k\) from (8) into the expression for the square root in the denominator of the natural logarithm in (19), we obtain:

$$\sqrt{b_0^2 + k^2} = \sqrt{b_0^2 + \frac {\alpha}{\beta}a_0^2 - b_0^2} = \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{\beta}}a_0$$

and hence (19) becomes:

$$\begin{equation} \ln \Bigg(\frac {b + \sqrt{b^2 + k^2}}{b_0 + \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{\beta}}a_0}\Bigg) = -\sqrt{\alpha \beta} \; t \end{equation}$$

from where it follows that:

$$\begin{equation} b + \sqrt{b^2 + k^2} = \Big(b_0 + \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{\beta}}a_0\Big) e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} \; t} \end{equation}$$

Since the right hand side of (21) does not depend on \(b\), let:

$$\begin{equation} \Big(b_0 + \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{\beta}}a_0\Big) e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} \; t} = C \end{equation}$$

in which case (21) becomes:

$$\begin{equation} b + \sqrt{b^2 + k^2} = C \end{equation}$$

Rearrange (23) as follows:

$$\begin{equation} \sqrt{b^2 + k^2} = C - b \end{equation}$$

and square both sides of (24):

$$b^2 + k^2 = C^2 - 2Cb + b^2$$ $$k^2 = C^2 - 2Cb$$ $$\begin{equation} b = \frac {C^2 - k^2}{2C} = \frac {C}{2} - \frac{k^2}{2C} \end{equation}$$

\(k^2\) from (8) can be rearranged as follows:

$$k^2 = \frac {\alpha}{\beta}a_0^2 - b_0^2 =$$ $$\Bigg(\sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}a_0 + b_0 \Bigg) \times \Bigg(\sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}a_0 - b_0 \Bigg)$$

from where for the last term in (25) we have:

$$\begin{equation} \frac{k^2}{2C} = \frac {1}{2}\Bigg(\sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}a_0 - b_0 \Bigg)e^{\sqrt{\alpha \beta} \; t} \end{equation}$$

Putting (26) and (22) into (25), we obtain for \(b(t)\):

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{b(t) = \frac {1}{2}\sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}} \Bigg(\Bigg(a_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0\Bigg)e^{-\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} - \Bigg(a_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0 \Bigg)e^{\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t}\Bigg)} \end{equation}$$

For \(a(t)\) we have from (2):

$$a(t) = - \frac {1}{\alpha} \frac {db(t)}{dt}$$

or from (1):

$$a(t) = - \beta \int b(t)dt$$

Either way:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{a(t) = \frac {1}{2}\Bigg(\Bigg(a_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0 \Bigg)e^{- \sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} + \Bigg(a_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0 \Bigg)e^{\sqrt{\alpha \beta} t} \Bigg)} \end{equation}$$

It follows then that for the total annihilation time \(t_a\) we can have either:

$$a(t_a) = 0$$

or:

$$b(t_a) = 0$$

For \(a(t_a) = 0\) from (28) it follows that:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{t_a(a = 0) = \frac {1}{2\sqrt{\alpha \beta}} \ln\Bigg(- \frac {a_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0}{a_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0}\Bigg)} \end{equation}$$

For \(b(t_a) = 0\) from (27) it follows that:

$$\begin{equation} \bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{t_a(b = 0) = \frac {1}{2\sqrt{\alpha \beta}} \ln\Bigg(\frac {a_0 + \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0}{a_0 - \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0}\Bigg)} \end{equation}$$

We observe that when the army \(B\) is annihilated then from (5) it follows that for the number of troops left in the army \(A\) to be not complex the following condition must hold:

$$a_0 > \sqrt{\frac {\beta}{\alpha}}b_0$$

in which case the natural logarithm in (29) is not defined while the natural logarithm in (30) is well defined.

Conversely, if the army \(A\) is totally annihilated then from (4) we have:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{b(a = 0) = \sqrt{b_0^2 - \frac {\alpha}{\beta}a_0^2}}$$

For the number of surviving troops in the army \(B\) to remain real the following condition must hold:

$$b_0 > \sqrt{\frac {\alpha}{\beta}}a_0$$

in which case the natural logarithm in (30) is not defined while the natural logarithm in (29) is well defined.

\(\blacksquare\)
