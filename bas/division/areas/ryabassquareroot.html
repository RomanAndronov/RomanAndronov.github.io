Square Root

Problem

Find the square area of a plane region delineated by the graph of a square root function \(y=\sqrt x\), the \(x\)-axis and a perpendicular to \(Ox\) through an arbitrary point B on the positive half of \(Ox\).


Right Sum Solution

The square are of the \(i-\)th primitive rectangle is:

$$A'_{i, r} = \Delta x \times \sqrt x_i =$$ $$\frac {l}{n} \times \sqrt {\frac {l}{n} \times i}$$ $$\frac {l^{\frac{3}{2}}}{n^{\frac{3}{2}}} \sqrt i$$

Right Riemann Sum is:

$$A'_r = \sum_{i = 1}^n A'_{i, r} =$$ $$\frac {l^{\frac{3}{2}}}{n^{\frac{3}{2}}} \sum_{i=1}^n \sqrt {i}$$

and:

$$A_r = \lim_{n\to +\infty} A'_r =$$ $$\lim_{n\to +\infty} \frac {l^{\frac{3}{2}}}{n^{\frac{3}{2}}} \sum_{i=1}^N \sqrt i =$$ $$l^{\frac{3}{2}} \lim_{n\to +\infty} \frac {1}{n^{\frac{3}{2}}} \sum_{i=1}^n \sqrt i$$

In Parabola chapter we have deduced the formula for the square area of a plane region under the graph of a square root function:

$$\begin{equation} A = \frac {2}{3}lh \end{equation}$$

It follows then that both limits, for left and right sums, exist and are equal. Based on that observation we can equate \(A_r\) to (1).

Adjusting the magnitude of the square area in question to our case by swapping the \(x\) and \(y\) axis and taking \(a = 1\), we obtain:

$$A = \frac {2}{3}lh$$ $$h = \sqrt l$$ $$A = \frac {2}{3}l^{\frac{3}{2}}$$

We can now equate the magnitudes of two square areas:

$$\begin{equation} l^{\frac{3}{2}} \lim_{n\to +\infty} \frac {1}{n^{\frac{3}{2}}} \sum_{i=1}^n \sqrt i = \frac {2}{3}l^{\frac{3}{2}} \end{equation}$$

or, after dividing both sides of (2) by \( l^{\frac{3}{2}} \):

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{\lim_{n\to +\infty} \frac {1}{n^{\frac{3}{2}}} \sum_{i=1}^n \sqrt i = \frac {2}{3}}$$

We observe that even though we do not know how to compute the above sum and (hence) the limit we still were able to find its value - an approach that we will use again in Circle chapter.

\(\blacksquare\)
