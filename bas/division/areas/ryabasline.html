Straight Line

Problem

Find the square area of a plane region delineated by the graph of a straight line function \(y = mx, m > 0\), the \(x\)-axis and a perpendicular to \(Ox\) through an arbitrary point \(B\) on \(Ox\):


Solution

Let:

$$OB = l$$ $$BP_n = h$$

For the

Left Sum

the square area of the \(i-\)th Left Riemann Rectangle is:

$$A'_{i, l} = y_i \times \Delta x =$$ $$y(x_{i-1}) \times \frac {l}{n}=$$ $$mx_{i-1} \times \frac {l}{n}=$$ $$m \frac {l}{n} \times (i - 1) \times \frac {l}{n}=$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{n^2} \times i - \frac {ml^2}{n^2}$$

Left Riemann Sum of square areas of all the Left Riemann Rectangles then is:

$$A'_l = \sum_{i=1}^n A'_{i, l} =$$ $$\sum_{i=1}^n \Big(\frac {ml^2}{n^2} \times i - \frac {ml^2}{n^2}\Big)=$$ $$\begin{equation} \frac {ml^2}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n i - \frac {ml^2}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n 1= \end{equation}$$ $$\begin{equation} \frac {ml^2}{n^2} \frac {n(n + 1)}{2} - \frac {ml^2}{n} \end{equation}$$

where in (1) we have used the formula for the sum of first powers of \(i\) (a uniform approach to computing these types of sums is covered in Scope Expansion section):

$$\begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^n i = \frac {n(n + 1)}{2} \end{equation}$$

If we factor out \(n\) from the numerator of (3):

$$n(n + 1) = n^2\Big(1 + \frac {1}{n}\Big)$$

then the \(n^2\)s in the numerator and the denominator of the first term in (2) cancel out:

$$A'_l = \frac {ml^2}{2n^2}n^2\Big(1 + \frac {1}{n}\Big) - \frac {ml^2}{n}=$$ $$\begin{equation} \frac {ml^2}{2} - \frac {ml^2}{2n} \end{equation}$$

Applying the limit to \(A'_l\) when \(n \to +\infty\) we obtain:

$$A_l = \lim_{n \to +\infty} A'_l =$$ $$\lim_{n \to +\infty} \Big( \frac {ml^2}{2} - \frac {ml^2}{2n}\Big)=$$ $$\lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {ml^2}{2} - \lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {ml^2}{2n} =$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{2} - \frac {ml^2}{2} \lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {1}{n}=$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{2} - 0 =$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{2}$$

From the given functional relationship \(y = mx\) it follows that if \(x = l\) then \(h = ml\) and:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{A_l = \frac {ml^2}{2} = \frac {h^2}{2m} = \frac {lh}{2}}$$

For the


Right Sum

the square area of the \(i-\)th Right Riemann Rectangle is:

$$A'_{i, r} = y_i \times \Delta x =$$ $$y(x_i) \times \frac {l}{n}=$$ $$mx_i \times \frac {l}{n}=$$ $$m \frac {l}{n} \times i \times \frac {l}{n}=$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{n^2} \times i$$

Right Riemann Sum of square areas of all the Right Riemann Rectangles then is:

$$A'_r = \sum_{i=1}^n A'_{i, r} =$$ $$\sum_{i=1}^n \Big(\frac {ml^2}{n^2} \times i\Big)=$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n i=$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{n^2} \frac {n(n + 1)}{2}=$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{n^2} \frac {n^2(1 + \frac {1}{n})}{2}=$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{2} + \frac {ml^2}{2n}$$

Applying the limit to \(A'_r\) when \(n \to +\infty\) we obtain:

$$A_r = \lim_{n \to +\infty} A'_r =$$ $$\lim_{n \to +\infty} \Big( \frac {ml^2}{2} + \frac {ml^2}{2n}\Big)=$$ $$\lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {ml^2}{2} + \lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {ml^2}{2n} =$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{2} + \frac {ml^2}{2} \lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {1}{n}=$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{2} + 0 =$$ $$\frac {ml^2}{2}$$

From the given functional relationship \(y = mx\) it follows that if \(x = l\) then \(h = ml\) and:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{A_r = \frac {ml^2}{2} = \frac {h^2}{2m} = \frac {lh}{2}}$$

We observe that since the limits of both sums exist and are equal, it follows that:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{A = A_l = A_r = \frac {ml^2}{2} = \frac {h^2}{2m} = \frac {lh}{2}}$$

It turns out that for a straight line a number of other arrangements of primitive rectangles also works.

Horizontal Inner Sum

For this type of sum we construct the primitive rectangles in an order reverse to the one used to construct the primitive rectangles for the traditional, left and right sums: divide the vertical line segment \(BP_n = h\) into \(n\) line segments of equal length:

$$\frac {h}{n}$$

These line segments will become the sides of the primitive rectangles.

Construct \(n\) perpendiculars to \(Oy\) - one \(h_i\) perpendicular through the corresponding point \(y_i\) on \(BP_n\). Each perpendicular \(h_i\) will intersect the graph of the function \(y = mx\) at a point \(P_i\).

Construct \(n\) perpendiculars to \(Ox\) - one \(v_i\) perpendicular through the corresponding point \(P_i\).

Finish constructing the primitive rectangles that are fully contained within \(\triangle OBP_n\) and number them in a bottom to top fashion as shown:

Then:

$$A' = \sum_{i=1}^n A'_i =$$ $$\sum_{i=1}^n w_i \times \frac {h}{n} =$$ $$\sum_{i=1}^n \Big(l - x_i\Big) \frac {h}{n} =$$ $$\begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^n \Big(l - \frac {l}{n} \times i\Big) \frac {h}{n} = \end{equation}$$ $$\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{lh}{n} - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac {lh}{n^2} \times i =$$ $$\frac{lh}{n} \times n - \frac {lh}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n i =$$ $$lh - \frac {lh}{n^2} \frac {n(n+1)}{2}=$$ $$lh - \frac {lh}{2} - \frac {lh}{2n}=$$ $$\begin{equation} \frac {lh}{2} - \frac {lh}{2n} \end{equation}$$

We observe that (4) and (6) are identical and hence:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{A = \frac {lh}{2}}$$


Diagonal Parallel Inner Sum

is a sum of square areas of primitive rectangles each of which is fully contained in \(\triangle OBP_n\) and is positioned in such a way that its long side is parallel to the straight line \(y = mx\):

in which case:

$$EC = \frac {h}{n}$$ $$DC = EC \times \cos(\alpha) = \frac {h}{n} \times \cos(\alpha)$$ $$x_iC = w_i = \frac {l - x_i}{\cos(\alpha)} = \frac {l - \frac {l}{n} \times i}{\cos(\alpha)}$$ $$A'_i = w_i \times DC = \frac {l - x_i}{\cos(\alpha)} \times \frac {h}{n} \times \cos(\alpha)$$ $$\begin{equation} A'_i = \Big(l - \frac {l}{n} \times i\Big) \frac {h}{n} \end{equation}$$

We observe that the terms in (5) and (7) are identical and hence:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{A = \frac {lh}{2}}$$
