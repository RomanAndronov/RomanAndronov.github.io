Straight Line Problem Solution

Problem

Prove that the limit of the sum of square areas of primitive rectangles arranged as shown below is equal to:

$$\frac {lh}{2}$$


Solution

Let:

$$OB = l$$ $$BP_n = h$$

Divide, for example, \(BP_n\) into \(n\) line segments of equal length and construct \(n - 1\) parallels to the straight line \(y = mx\) through each \(y_i\), see the drawing above:

$$y_{i-1}y_i = \Delta y = \frac {h}{n}$$

These parallels will intersect \(Ox\) in \(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_{n - 1}\) points cutting \(OB\) into \(n\) line segments of equal length:

$$x_{i-1}x_i = \Delta x = \frac {l}{n}$$

Construct \(n - 1\) perpendiculars to \(y = mx\) through \(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_{n - 1}\). These perpendiculars will delineate \(n - 2\) primitive rectangles with equal width as measured along the straight line \(y = mx\):

$$\frac {l}{n}\cos(\alpha)$$

and varying height as measured along the perpendicular to \(y = mx\):

$$\frac {l}{n}\sin(\alpha) \times i$$

Consequently, numbering such rectangles in a left to right fashion, the square area of the \(i-\)th such rectangle will be:

$$\frac {l^2}{n^2}\sin(\alpha)\cos(\alpha) \times i, \quad x \in [1, n-2]$$

The square area of a "center" rectangle whose one vertex is at \(x_{n-1}\) and the other is at \(y_1\) will be:

$$(n - 1)\frac {l}{n}\sin(\alpha) \times \frac {l}{n\cos(\alpha)} =$$ $$(n - 1)\frac {l^2}{n^2}\tan(\alpha) =$$ $$(n - 1)\frac {ml^2}{n^2}$$

If we number the primitive rectangles constructed along the vertical \(BP_n\) starting from \(P_n\) and moving along \(y = mx\) towards \(O\) then the square area of the \(i-\)th such rectangle will be:

$$\frac {h^2}{n^2}\sin(\alpha)\cos(\alpha) \times i, \quad x \in [1, n-2]$$

making a total number of primitive rectangles \(2n - 3\).

Summing the square areas of all of the primitive rectangles we obtain:

$$\begin{equation} A' = \frac {l^2 + h^2}{n^2}\sin(\alpha)\cos(\alpha) \sum_{i=1}^{n - 2}i + ml^2 \frac{n - 1}{n^2} \end{equation}$$

From the previous exercises we already know that in the limiting case when \(n \to +\infty\) the last term in (1) vanishes so we can safely throw it out.

Rearrange the factor in front of the sum of the remaining term:

$$\Big(l^2 + h^2\Big)\sin(\alpha)\cos(\alpha)$$

as follows:

$$l^2 + h^2 = \sqrt{l^2 + h^2} \times \sqrt{l^2 + h^2}$$

to obtain:

$$\Big(l^2 + h^2\Big)\sin(\alpha)\cos(\alpha)=$$ $$\sqrt{l^2 + h^2} \times \cos(\alpha) \times \sqrt{l^2 + h^2} \times \sin(\alpha)=$$ $$\begin{equation} l \times h \end{equation}$$

Put (2) into (1):

$$A' = \frac {lh}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n - 2}i =$$ $$\frac {lh}{n^2} \frac {(n - 2)(n - 1)}{2} =$$ $$\frac {lh}{n^2}\frac {n^2(1 - \frac {2}{n})(1 - \frac {1}{n})}{2} =$$ $$\frac {lh}{2}\Big(1 - \frac {3}{n} + \frac {2}{n^2}\Big)$$

Apply the limit to \(A'\) when \(n \to +\infty\):

$$A = \lim_{n \to +\infty} A' =$$ $$\lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {lh}{2} - \frac {3lh}{2} \lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {1}{n} + lh \lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {1}{n^2}=$$ $$\frac {lh}{2}$$

What was required to prove.

\(\blacksquare\)
