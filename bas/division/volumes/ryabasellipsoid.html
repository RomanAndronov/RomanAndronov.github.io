Ellipsoid

Problem

Find the volume of an ellipsoid with the semi-axes \(a, b\) and \(c\) assuming, without the loss of generality, that:

$$c > a > b$$


Solution

Use thin right elliptic cylinders as primitive volumes positioned as shown in the drawing and consider the ellipsoid's corresponding half.

The height \(h_i\) of the \(i-\)th primitive cylinder as measured along the \(z\) axis is:

$$h_i = \frac {c}{n}$$

The volume of the \(i-\)th primitive cylinder is:

$$V'_i = \pi x_i \times y_i \times h_i =$$ $$\pi \times x_i \times y_i \times \frac {c}{n}$$

where \(x_i\) and \(y_i\) are the semi-axes of the \(i-\)th primitive cylinder.

To express the values of \(x_i\) and \(y_i\) in given terms we observe the following.

If we cut the ellipsoid with the \(zOx\) plane then the resulting cross section will be bound by the largest ellipse that can be constructed on the surface of this particular ellipsoid - an equivalent of Great Circle on a sphere. The canonical equation of this Great Ellipse of sort is:

$$\begin{equation} \frac {x^2}{a^2} + \frac {z^2}{c^2} = 1 \end{equation}$$

Since the point \(x_i\) is located on the perimeter of the above ellipse, from (1) it follows that for the corresponding \(z_i\) we have:

$$\frac {x_i^2}{a^2} + \frac {z_i^2}{c^2} = 1$$

But:

$$z_i = \frac {c}{n} \times i$$

and hence:

$$x_i = \frac {a}{n} \sqrt {n^2 - i^2}$$

Similarly, by cutting the ellipsoid with the \(zOy\) plane we obtain \(y_i\):

$$y_i = \frac {b}{n} \sqrt {n^2 - i^2}$$

The volume of the \(i-\)th primitive cylinder then is:

$$V'_i = \pi \frac {abc}{n^3}(n^2 - i^2)$$

The volume of the entire ellipsoid is the sum of volumes of two of its halves:

$$V' = 2 \times \sum_{n = 1}^n V'_i =$$ $$\frac {2\pi abc}{n^3}\sum_{i = 1}^n(n^2 - i^2) =$$ $$\frac {2\pi abc}{n^3}\Big(\sum_{i = 1}^n n^2 -\sum_{i = 1}^n i^2\Big) =$$ $$\frac {2\pi abc}{n^3}\Bigg(n^3 - \frac {n(n + 1)(2n + 1)}{6}\Bigg) =$$ $$\frac {2\pi abc}{n^3}\Bigg(n^3 - \frac {n^3(1 + \frac {1}{n})(2 + \frac {1}{n})}{6}\Bigg) =$$ $$\frac {\pi abc}{3}\Big(4 - \frac {3}{n} - \frac {1}{n^2}\Big)$$

where again, as in Sphere chapter, the index \(i\) runs to \(n\) since the height of the \(n-\)th cylinder is zero:

$$V = \lim_{n \to +\infty} V' =$$ $$\lim_{n \to +\infty}\frac {\pi abc}{3}\Big(4 - \frac {3}{n} - \frac {1}{n^2}\Big) =$$ $$\frac {\pi abc}{3}\Big(4 - \lim_{n \to +\infty}\frac {3}{n} - \lim_{n \to +\infty} \frac {1}{n^2}\Big) =$$ $$\frac {4\pi abc}{3}$$

And hence:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{V = \frac {4}{3} \pi abc}$$

\(\blacksquare\)
