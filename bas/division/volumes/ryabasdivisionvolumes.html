Volumes

The pages that follow are a demonstration of how the Division basic approach may be applied to the problem of calculating the volumes of certain three-dimensional solids without explicit integration.


Problem

Adding to all the working assumptions of Square Areas chapter the fact that the volume of a right rectangular prism with sides \(a, b\) and \(c\) is:

$$a \times b \times c$$

find the volume of a given three-dimensional solid.


Solution Template

We will use an orthogonal \(xyz\) coordinate system with origin at \(O\). We will divide a given solid into an arbitrary number of primitive parts whose volume we do know how to compute. In Square Areas chapters we used rectangles as primitive parts exclusively. In Volumes chapters we will have to be more inventive - we will employ right rectangular and triangular prisms and right circular and elliptic cylinders.

We will choose the size and the orientation of the primitive parts in such a way that their total volume will be larger (\(V_u\)) and smaller (\(V_l\)) than the volume sought after. We then will begin the iterative process of decreasing one dimension of each existing primitive part while adding new appropriately sized and positioned primitive parts. We will capture this process mathematically as:

$$n \to +\infty$$

where \(n\) stands for the number of primitive parts in the current iteration. We will then argue that if:

$$\lim_{n \to +\infty} V_u = \lim_{n \to +\infty} V_l = V$$

then \(V\) is the volume of the three-dimensional solid in question and the problem is solved.
