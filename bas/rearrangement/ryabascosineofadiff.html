\(\cos (\alpha -\beta)\)

Problem

Express the cosine of a composite angle \(\alpha - \beta\) in terms of trigonometric functions of primitive angles \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\).


Solution

The arrangement of the right triangles that allows us to deduce the identity for the cosine of a difference of two angles is the same one used to deduce the corresponding identity for the sine of a difference of two angles:

By definition we have:

$$\cos (\alpha - \beta) = \frac {AF}{AE} =$$ $$\frac {AC + CF}{AE} =$$ $$\frac {AC + GE}{AE} =$$ $$\frac {AC}{AE} + \frac {GE}{AE}$$

\(AC\) is a side of the right triangle \(\triangle ACB\) and \(AE\) is a hypotenuse of the right triangle \(\triangle ABE\) and both of these right, by construction, triangles share \(AB\):

$$\frac {AC}{AE} = \frac {AC}{AE} \times 1 =$$ $$\frac {AC}{AE} \times \frac {AB}{AB} =$$ $$\frac {AC}{AB} \times \frac {AB}{AE}$$ $$\frac {AC}{AB} = \cos \alpha$$ $$\frac {AB}{AE} = \cos \beta$$ $$\frac {AC}{AE} = \cos \alpha \cos \beta$$ $$\cos (\alpha - \beta) = \cos \alpha \cos \beta + \frac {GE}{AE}$$

Consequently, \(GE\) is a side of the right triangle \(BGE\) and \(AE\) is hypotenuse of the right triangle \(ABE\) and both of these right triangles share \(BE\):

$$\frac {GE}{AE} = \frac {GE}{AE} \times 1 =$$ $$\frac {GE}{AE} \times \frac {BE}{BE} =$$ $$\frac {GE}{BE} \times \frac {BE}{AE}$$ $$\frac {GE}{BE} = \sin \alpha$$ $$\frac {BE}{AE} = \sin \beta$$ $$\frac {GE}{AE} = \sin \alpha \sin \beta$$

and:

$$\bbox[#e8e8e8,3pt]{\cos (\alpha - \beta) = \cos \alpha \cos \beta + \sin \alpha \sin \beta}$$

\(\blacksquare\)
